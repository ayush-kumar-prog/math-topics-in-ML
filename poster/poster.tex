\begin{block}{From the textbook U-curve to the double-descent peak}
\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]
\begin{column}{0.58\linewidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth,height=7.0cm,keepaspectratio]{assets/errorvscomplexity.png}\\[0.1ex]
{\scriptsize\color{uommuted}\textbf{Fig.~1, Nakkiran et al.\ (2019).} \textbf{Left:} test error on ResNet18s, varying width, CIFAR-10 + 15\% label noise. \textbf{Right:} test error coloured by training epoch; dashed red curve = optimal early stopping.}
\end{column}\hspace{0.02\linewidth}%
\begin{column}{0.38\linewidth}
\eyebrow{The thesis in one line}
{\color{uomgrey}\textbf{For modern training procedures the population-risk curve has \emph{two} descents,} separated by a sharp peak at the \kw{interpolation threshold}, where the procedure can just fit the train set.}\\[0.4ex]
\eyebrow{Beyond Section 5.3}
\small
The lecture introduces the phenomenon and links it to implicit regularisation. We go further by:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.15ex}
\item Replacing \emph{parameter count} with \kw{Effective Model Complexity} (EMC), the capacity of the \emph{whole training procedure} \(\T\).
\item Showing the second descent appears \kw{model-wise}, \kw{epoch-wise} \emph{and} \kw{sample-wise}.
\item Stating the \kw{Generalised Double Descent Hypothesis} and the limits of its current justification.
\end{itemize}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}

% =====================================================================
% TWO-COLUMN ROW: Setting & Notation | Definition & Hypothesis

\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]

% --------- LEFT COLUMN ---------
\begin{column}{0.485\linewidth}

\begin{block}{Setting: the lecture decomposition we extend}
Following the course notation (Notes \S\,5.1, Eqs.~68 to 73):
\[\hat f \leftarrow \mathcal{A}(\F;\Sset),\qquad
  R(f) := \E_{\bm{xy}}[\ell(\bm{y},f(\bm{x}))],\qquad
  \widehat R(f,\Sset) := \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ell(\bm{y}_i,f(\bm{x}_i)).\]
The excess risk decomposes into three statistically meaningful gaps:
\[\underbrace{R(\hat f) - R(y^{*})}_{\text{excess risk}}
  = \underbrace{R(\hat f) - R(f_{\mathrm{erm}})}_{\text{\color{accentteal}\textbf{optimisation}}}
  + \underbrace{R(f_{\mathrm{erm}}) - R(f^{*})}_{\text{\color{accentgold}\textbf{estimation}}}
  + \underbrace{R(f^{*}) - R(y^{*})}_{\text{\color{accentblue}\textbf{approximation}}}.\]
The classical bias-variance trade-off bundles \emph{approximation + part of estimation} into \kw{bias} and the rest into \kw{variance}; what the lecture summary calls \emph{incomplete}, not wrong.

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.2ex}
\item \kw{Lecture U-curve} (Notes Fig.~17) holds \(\F,\mathcal{A}\) fixed and grows \(\F\).
\item \kw{Paper's pivot:} treat the \emph{whole procedure} \(\T\!=\!(\F,\mathcal{A},\text{epochs},\text{reg.},\text{labels})\) as the single object whose capacity matters, so optimisation, estimation, and labels jointly determine the peak.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}

\end{column}

% --------- RIGHT COLUMN ---------
\begin{column}{0.485\linewidth}

\begin{block}{Definition + Hypothesis (Nakkiran et al.\ 2019)}
\eyebrow{Definition 1: Effective Model Complexity}
A \kw{training procedure} \(\T\) maps any labelled train set \(S = \{(\bm{x}_i,\bm{y}_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}\) to a classifier \(\T(S)\). For an error tolerance \(\varepsilon>0\),
\[\boxed{\;\mathrm{EMC}_{\D,\varepsilon}(\T)\;:=\;\max\bigl\{\,n\;\bigm|\;\E_{S\sim\D^{n}}\bigl[\mathrm{Error}_{S}(\T(S))\bigr]\le\varepsilon\,\bigr\}\;}\]
i.e.\ the largest sample size on which \(\T\) can still drive train error below \(\varepsilon\). Note that \(\mathrm{EMC}\) depends on the optimiser, training time, augmentation and labels, not just on the architecture.

\vspace{0.4ex}
\eyebrow{Hypothesis 1: Generalised Double Descent}
For ``natural'' \(\D\), neural-network procedures \(\T\), and small \(\varepsilon\) (the paper uses \(\varepsilon=0.1\)):
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.3ex}
\item[\chip{accentteal}{$\mathrm{EMC}\!\ll\! n$}] \kw{Under-parameterised:} any change to \(\T\) that \emph{increases} EMC \emph{decreases} test error.
\item[\chip{accentcoral}{$\mathrm{EMC}\!\approx\! n$}] \kw{Critically parameterised:} the same change \emph{may decrease or increase} test error. This is the danger zone.
\item[\chip{accentgreen}{$\mathrm{EMC}\!\gg\! n$}] \kw{Over-parameterised:} any change to \(\T\) that \emph{increases} EMC \emph{decreases} test error.
\end{itemize}

\HypBox{\textbf{Why this is a hypothesis, not a theorem.} \(\varepsilon\) and the words ``sufficiently smaller / larger'' are heuristic; the critical-interval width is not formally specified for deep nets.}
\end{block}

\end{column}
\end{columns}

\eyebrow{Reading the heatmaps}
\small
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.3ex}
\item \kw{Same axes} for both: ResNet18 width parameter \(k\!\in\![1,64]\) (\(k\!=\!64\) is the standard ResNet18) versus training epochs (log).
\item \kw{Horizontal slice} \(\Rightarrow\) \chip{accentcoral}{model-wise}: peak as width grows.
\item \kw{Vertical slice} \(\Rightarrow\) \chip{accentteal}{epoch-wise}: peak as training continues.
\item \kw{Key alignment:} the bright \emph{test} ridge (left) sits on top of the \emph{train} interpolation curve (right), so the peak lies \emph{at} the threshold, not far past it.
\end{itemize}
\eyebrow{Setup (paper \S\,4)}
\footnotesize
ResNet18, CIFAR-10, \kw{15\% label noise}, data augmentation, Adam (lr 1e-4), up to \(4\)k epochs. Widths \([k,2k,4k,8k]\) for \(k\!\in\![1,64]\). Identical results hold under SGD and on CIFAR-100; the peak is \emph{also} present without label noise (Fig.~4a, paper).
\end{column}

\vspace{0.2ex}
\takeaway{The unifying variable is EMC, not parameter count. Vary anything in \(\T\) that moves EMC across \(n\) and the peak moves with it.}
\end{block}

\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]

% --------- LEFT: SAMPLE-WISE ---------
\begin{column}{0.485\linewidth}
\begin{block}{Sample-wise: more data can locally hurt}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth,height=5.4cm,keepaspectratio]{assets/NLP-small-data-vary-MDD.png}\\[0.1ex]
{\scriptsize\color{uommuted}\textbf{Fig.~3, Nakkiran et al.} IWSLT'14 De-En, 6-layer Transformer, \(d_{\text{ff}}\!=\!4d_{\text{model}}\), 80k gradient steps, 10\% label smoothing. Bars highlight \(d_{\text{model}}\) range where 4.5\(\times\) more data \emph{worsens} test loss.}
\justifying
\vspace{0.1ex}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.2ex}
\item Fixing \((\T,\F)\) and increasing \(n\) leaves the interpolation threshold in EMC space unchanged, but \emph{shifts \(n\)} relative to it.
\item Widths near the small-sample threshold get \emph{pushed into} the critical regime by adding data.
\item Local non-monotonicity only: asymptotically, more data still wins.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{column}

% --------- RIGHT: MECHANISM ---------
\begin{column}{0.485\linewidth}
\begin{block}{Mechanism (intuition; rigorous only for linear models)}
\eyebrow{Critically parameterised} \(\;\) Effectively \emph{one} interpolating model. Forcing it to fit any noisy/misspecified label \emph{destroys global structure} \(\Rightarrow\) test error spikes.

\vspace{0.3ex}
\eyebrow{Over-parameterised} \(\;\) Many interpolants. The optimiser's \kw{implicit bias} (e.g.\ SGD toward small-norm) lets it ``absorb'' noise while keeping the on-distribution map smooth.

\vspace{0.5ex}
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[every node/.style={font=\footnotesize},x=0.32\linewidth,y=0.85cm]
  \shade[left color=accentteal!20, right color=accentcoral!55] (0,0) rectangle (1,0.55);
  \shade[left color=accentcoral!55, right color=accentgreen!25] (1,0) rectangle (2.5,0.55);
  \draw[->,thick] (0,-0.05) -- (2.6,-0.05) node[right] {\color{uommuted}\footnotesize\(\mathrm{EMC}\) increasing};
  \node[anchor=south] at (0.5,0.55) {\bfseries few solutions};
  \node[anchor=south] at (1.0,0.65) {\bfseries\color{accentcoral}\(\approx n\)};
  \node[anchor=south] at (1.75,0.55) {\bfseries many solutions};
  \node[anchor=north] at (0.5,0) {U-curve helps};
  \node[anchor=north,color=accentcoral] at (1.0,-0.15) {peak};
  \node[anchor=north] at (1.75,0) {SGD bias chooses well};
\end{tikzpicture}

\justifying
\vspace{0.4ex}
\eyebrow{Where this is rigorous}
\(\;\)Mei \& Montanari (2019), Belkin et al.\ (2019) prove the test-risk peak for overparameterised linear regression with mis-specification; Bartlett et al.\ (2020) prove benign overfitting under min-norm interpolation. The deep-net analogue is conjectured, not proved.
\end{block}
\end{column}

\begin{columns}[T,onlytextwidth]

% --------- LEFT: WHAT WE DON'T CLAIM ---------
\begin{column}{0.485\linewidth}
\begin{block}{What we do \emph{not} claim}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.25ex}
\item ``Bias-variance is wrong.'' \textbf{No}: it is correct within a fixed class, but \emph{incomplete} once \(\T\) varies.
\item ``Bigger/longer/more data is always better.'' \textbf{No}: each can be \emph{worse} in the critical interval.
\item ``Label noise causes the peak.'' Noise \emph{amplifies} it; peak appears at 0\% noise too (Figs.~4a, 7) under model mis-specification.
\item ``Parameters \(=\) capacity.'' Parameters are one input to EMC; epochs and regularisation matter as much.
\item ``EMC is a finished theory.'' \(\varepsilon\) and ``sufficiently smaller/larger'' are heuristic; deep-net mechanism is conjectured.
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{column}

% --------- RIGHT: PRACTICE + OPEN ---------
\begin{column}{0.485\linewidth}
\begin{block}{Practical message \& open questions}
\eyebrow{For practitioners}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.2ex}
\item \kw{Validate}: train and test curves \emph{uncouple} near the interpolation threshold.
\item \kw{Avoid the critical regime}: early stopping, weight decay, ensembling (Fig.~28), or scale past the threshold.
\item \kw{Data augmentation shifts the peak right} (Fig.~5): a free way to escape it.
\end{itemize}
\eyebrow{Open scientific questions}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.2ex}
\item Principled \(\varepsilon\); closed-form critical-interval width.
\item A non-linear EMC theorem for over-parameterised nets.
\item SGD implicit bias for realistic architectures and losses.
\end{itemize}
\takeaway{Interpolation is a threshold to understand, not a final verdict.}
\end{block}
\end{column}
